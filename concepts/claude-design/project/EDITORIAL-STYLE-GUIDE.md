# Kickabout — Editorial Style Guide

A reference for writing match previews, recaps, and features in the Kickabout voice.
Hand this to Claude (or any writer) as context before drafting new content.

---

## 1. Who we're writing for

Casual-to-intermediate World Cup viewers — not hardcore tactics nerds, not total
newcomers. They know the big names and the big nations, enjoy a strong opinion, and
want to feel smart without being lectured. Assume curiosity, not expertise.

**Tone in one line:** a knowledgeable friend at the pub who happens to write well —
confident, warm, a little wry, never condescending.

---

## 2. Voice principles

- **Plain English over jargon.** Keep the authority, lose the vocabulary tax. Say
  "how dangerous the squad is in front of goal" rather than "npxG per 90." If a
  technical term earns its place (Elo, xG), gloss it in passing.
- **Have a take.** Every piece commits to a view — who's favoured, what matters, what
  to watch. Hedging is boring. Be willing to say "Expect Brazil to win it; don't
  expect them to enjoy it."
- **Concrete beats vague.** Name players, clubs, venues, scorelines, specific matchups
  (e.g. "the Davies-versus-Modiba flank"). Specificity is what makes writing feel
  human and reported rather than generated.
- **Earn every sentence.** No filler, no throat-clearing, no "in today's fast-paced
  world of football." Cut anything that doesn't add a fact or a view.
- **Light wit, used sparingly.** One good dry line per piece lands; five is a comedy
  routine. Let the closing line carry the personality.

---

## 3. Structure

### Match preview (~3–4 short paragraphs)
1. **Hook** — why this tie is worth your time (the storyline, the stakes, the styles).
2. **The case for team A** — key men, form, what they do well.
3. **The case for team B** — same, the counter-argument.
4. **The verdict** — who's favoured and the one thing that decides it. End on a line
   with a point of view.

### Match recap (~3 short paragraphs)
1. **What it meant** — lead with significance, not the scoreline. ("Some results feel
   like a turning point.")
2. **How it happened** — the decisive performances, the moments, the player who ran it.
3. **The takeaway** — what we learned, what it means going forward. A pointed close.

### Feature / big-picture (~3 paragraphs)
A wider lens — where the tournament stands, a theme, a trend. Same voice; can be a
touch more essayistic. Still anchored in real specifics.

---

## 4. Mechanics

- **Length:** previews 180–260 words; recaps 140–200; features 220–320. Short
  paragraphs (2–4 sentences). White space is your friend.
- **Headline:** evocative + specific. Pair an image with the matchup —
  "Samba meets the machine: Brazil vs Japan is the tie of the round."
- **Dek (standfirst):** one sentence that sets up the hook and the stakes. Sentence
  case, no full stop needed if it reads as a label.
- **Spelling:** British/international football English — "favoured," "defence,"
  "kick-off," "the knockouts," "side" (not "team" every time).
- **Numbers:** spell out under ten in prose ("three weeks in"); use figures for
  scores, ages, ratings, rankings ("6-0," "at 40," "#3 in the world").
- **Players:** full name on first mention, surname after. Use the name the casual fan
  knows (Neymar, not Neymar Jr. da Silva Santos).

---

## 5. Grounding & accuracy (important)

- **Build on real data.** Pull from the actual squads, standings, results, coaches and
  ratings in the platform. True specifics give the writing authority.
- **Mark the line between fact and narrative.** Squads, scores, fixtures, ratings = real.
  Colour like "looked sharp," "ran the show," "a late winner" in a *preview* is
  informed speculation — fine for editorial, but flag it as such and replace with
  reported detail once a match is actually played or live data arrives.
- **Never invent hard facts.** No made-up scorelines, transfer news, injuries, or
  quotes. If you don't know it, write around it.
- **This is editorial, not betting advice.** Predictions come from a transparent model
  (team rating + form + squad + home advantage). Say so; never imply certainty.

---

## 6. Do / Don't

**Do**
- "Morocco don't do easy exits."
- "They beat Scotland 3-0 without getting out of second gear."
- "If Canada win the Davies flank, they win the match."

**Don't**
- "In what promises to be an exciting encounter between two great teams…"
- "Only time will tell who will come out on top."
- "Football is the beautiful game, and today is no exception."

---

## 7. Quick prompt to reuse

> Write a [preview/recap] of [Team A] vs [Team B] in the Kickabout voice: casual-to-
> intermediate audience, plain English, a clear point of view, concrete specifics
> (real players, clubs, form, venue). [preview: hook → case for A → case for B →
> verdict, ~220 words. recap: meaning → how it happened → takeaway, ~170 words.]
> Squads/scores/ratings are real; match colour is informed editorial, not reported
> fact. End on a line with a take.
