Every World Cup produces one number that stops you. This time it is this: Cape Verde, a nation of roughly half a million people, at their first World Cup, are into the knockout rounds. No smaller country has ever managed it. And their reward is the holders.

Our model is blunt about the mismatch, 96% Argentina, and the market only slightly kinder at 82%, which leaves Cape Verde a 5% to 18% puncher's chance depending on where you look. Those numbers are correct. They are also not the whole truth, because Cape Verde did not stumble here. They earned it, unbeaten, with three draws that tell you exactly how they will approach this: 0-0 with Spain, 2-2 with Uruguay, 0-0 with Saudi Arabia. That is not luck. That is a plan.

Bubista, the reigning CAF Coach of the Year, has built a compact, disciplined 4-2-3-1 that defends in two banks, keeps 40-year-old goalkeeper Vozinha busy and calm, and springs fast when it wins the ball. Holding Spain to a goalless draw is the sort of result that makes a coach's reputation. Ryan Mendes, the captain, carries the tournament serenity you cannot manufacture. If any minnow left in the draw can frustrate a superpower for an hour, it is this one.

The trouble is that Argentina are not Spain. Where the Euro champions have looked laboured, Lionel Scaloni's side have been the most coherent team in the tournament, a fluid 4-3-3 that morphs into a 4-2-3-1 without losing its shape, with Alexis Mac Allister, Enzo Fernández and Rodrigo De Paul controlling the tempo and Lionel Messi freed to roam and pick locks. They won their group with a perfect three from three and did it comfortably, Messi scoring twice against Austria and again against Jordan before Scaloni rested him with nine changes, a luxury nobody else can afford.

And Messi is the specific problem Cape Verde cannot solve. He arrives at this tie having just passed Miroslav Klose as the World Cup's all-time leading scorer, having found the net in seven consecutive World Cup matches, and, crucially, fully fit, the muscle fatigue that earned him a rest against Jordan nothing more than management. A deep, organised block is a fine defence against a team that needs to build its way through you. It is a much weaker defence against a player who only needs one half-yard of space to end the game with a single pass.

So how does it play out? Not the massacre the 96% implies. We expect Cape Verde to do what they have done all tournament, sit deep, stay compact, make Argentina patient, and keep the score down for the first hour. And then we expect Argentina's quality to tell, because over 90 minutes it always does against this kind of opponent: a Messi moment, a set piece, a Lautaro Martínez finish, and the dam breaks. Our call is a two-goal Argentina win, more grind than glory, the underdog dignified rather than humiliated.

The romantic in you wants the one shot, the smash-and-grab, the header from a corner that Vozinha's heroics protect to the final whistle. It is not impossible. It is simply the least likely outcome on a card full of live upsets elsewhere. Cape Verde's tournament has already been a triumph; anything here is a bonus written in the history books.

None of this means Cape Verde are harmless. Their whole tournament has been a lesson in making superior teams uncomfortable, and they carry a real threat on set pieces and second balls, where organisation matters more than pedigree. If they nick an early goal and make Argentina chase, the champions' aging spine, Nicolás Otamendi at the back, the reliance on Messi to conjure rather than dominate, becomes a live question rather than a footnote. The likeliest script is still Argentine control, but the margin between a nervy hour and a genuine scare is one Cape Verde corner, and Bubista knows it.

Look further ahead and the reason to watch Argentina closely is not this tie but the next. The winner drops into a Round of 16 against Australia or Egypt, an eminently winnable route that could carry Scaloni's side deep into the tournament without a serious scare. That is the danger for a champion: soft draws let you coast until the day the football gods hand you France or Spain and you discover, too late, that you never found top gear. Argentina should win this at a canter. The more interesting question is whether an easy fortnight is quietly the worst thing that can happen to them.
