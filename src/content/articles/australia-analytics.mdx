Australia came into the 2026 World Cup as a seeded side with real quality, seeded 28 in the world and rated 1800 on Elo. Tony Popovic's side advanced as group runners-up, winning 1, drawing 2 and losing 1 of 4 games while scoring 3 and conceding 3. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

## How they play

Their identity is control and containment, low-event games decided by the odd moment they are set up to provide. Across the games tracked they averaged 0.70 expected goals for and 0.90 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

## The campaign, game by game

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they won 2-0 against Turkiye, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (1.2 to 1.4 on xG). The scoreline was kinder than the performance, and they will know it. In their second group game they lost 0-2 against United States, and they were out-created, 0.3 to 1.1 on xG. No arguments about the result; they were beaten by the better side on the day. In their third group game they drew 0-0 against Paraguay, and it stayed tight on xG, 0.6 to 0.2. An even game that an even result did justice to. In the round-of-32 tie they were held to a 1-1 draw by Egypt and went out on penalties; nothing separated the sides across 120 minutes, and the shootout fell the other way.

## Attack

In attack the output matched the process, 3 goals from around 2.1 expected, which is the healthiest place to be: winning on repeatable chance-creation rather than hot streaks. Their most threatening display came against Turkiye, worth 1.2 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Jackson Irvine (14), Mathew Leckie (14), Harry Souttar (11), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

## Defence

Defensively they were miserly, conceding 3 and allowing only about 2.7 expected goals in the tracked games. A back line that gives up so little turns knockout football into a coin toss it can win from a single set piece. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Turkiye, who worked 1.4 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 2 clean sheets from 4 games.

In this tournament the scoring has leaned on Nestory Irankunda, whose 1 make up over half of the group-stage goals. That is production and dependency in one number: mark him out and the supply can dry up.

## The key men

Individually, Connor Metcalfe has been the driver, 1 goal in 3 appearances at an average match rating of 7.07. Sides that lean on one player peak when he does and dip when he is quiet, and that dependence is worth watching in a knockout.

The team news matters too. Availability doubts hang over J. Italiano (adductor injury), and how many of those clear up before kickoff shapes what this side can actually put on the pitch.

## What the numbers say

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers sit close to even, the mark of a side that has to fight for every result rather than one that overwhelms. A goal difference of +0 from 4 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

Set against the other 47 teams in the field, their attack ranks 37th for expected goals created and 33rd for goals scored, while the defence ranks 18th for expected goals allowed. That is the truer placing, stripped of who they happened to draw, and it is the number to hold onto when the bracket starts throwing up mismatches and upsets.

## The reckoning

Their tournament is over. It ended in the round of 32, a shootout exit after a 1-1 draw to Egypt on 3 July. There was nothing to separate the sides across 120 minutes; penalties decided it. The profile above is the record they leave behind, and the underlying numbers say they were closer to the level than the exit alone suggests.
