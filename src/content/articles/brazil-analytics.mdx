On the FIFA ranking Brazil sit 5, and the Elo model has them at 2009, which frames them as one of the tournament favourites. Carlo Ancelotti's side topped their group, winning 3, drawing 1 and losing 1 of 5 games while scoring 10 and conceding 4. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

## How they play

This is a front-foot side that both makes chances and denies them, the profile of a team built to go deep. Across the games tracked they averaged 2.19 expected goals for and 0.72 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

## The campaign, game by game

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they drew 1-1 against Morocco, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (1.3 to 1.4 on xG). An even game that an even result did justice to. In their second group game they won 3-0 against Haiti, and they carried the greater threat, 1.8 to 0.2 on xG. A statement result that left little room for argument. In their third group game they won 3-0 against Scotland, and the openings fell their way, 4.3 expected goals to 1.0. The margin flattered no one; they were that far ahead. In the round-of-32 tie they won 2-1 against Japan, and the expected-goals split (1.7 to 0.2) says they made the better chances. A win earned rather than gifted, and taken with the composure the occasion asked for. In the round-of-16 tie they lost 1-2 to Norway, and the expected-goals split (1.9 to 0.7) says they made the better chances. They made the better chances and still went home, which is the cruelty of a single knockout tie.

## Attack

In attack the output matched the process, 10 goals from around 11.0 expected, which is the healthiest place to be: winning on repeatable chance-creation rather than hot streaks. Their most threatening display came against Scotland, worth 4.3 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Neymar (79), Lucas Paquetá (13), Raphinha (11), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

## Defence

Defensively they were miserly, conceding 4 and allowing only about 3.6 expected goals in the tracked games. A back line that gives up so little turns knockout football into a coin toss it can win from a single set piece. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Morocco, who worked 1.4 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 2 clean sheets from 5 games.

In this tournament the scoring has leaned on Vinícius Júnior, whose 4 make up over half of the group-stage goals. That is production and dependency in one number: mark him out and the supply can dry up.

## The key men

Individually, Vinícius Júnior has been the driver, 4 goals and 1 assist in 4 appearances at an average match rating of 8.22. Sides that lean on one player peak when he does and dip when he is quiet, and that dependence is worth watching in a knockout.

The team news matters too. Availability doubts hang over Raphinha (hamstring injury), Neymar (calf injury), and how many of those clear up before kickoff shapes what this side can actually put on the pitch.

## What the numbers say

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers back the results: they created meaningfully more than they allowed, and that gap is usually the difference between a good run and an early flight home. A goal difference of +6 from 7 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

Set against the other 47 teams in the field, their attack ranks 1st for expected goals created and 5th for goals scored, while the defence ranks 8th for expected goals allowed. That is the truer placing, stripped of who they happened to draw, and it is the number to hold onto when the bracket starts throwing up mismatches and upsets.

## The reckoning

Their tournament is over. It ended in the last 16, a 1-2 defeat to Norway on 5 July. On the expected-goals evidence they had the better of it and can feel hard done by. The profile above is the record they leave behind, and the underlying numbers say they were closer to the level than the exit alone suggests.
