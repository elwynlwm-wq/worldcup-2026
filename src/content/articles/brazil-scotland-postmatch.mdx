## How it unfolded

Brazil did not need to be brilliant, and they were not. Under Carlo Ancelotti, that is starting to look like the point.

Vinícius Júnior settled it early, a seventh-minute finish of the kind that makes full-backs question their life choices, and doubled his tally on the stroke of half-time to take the tie out of Scotland's reach. Matheus Cunha added a third just after the hour, and from there Brazil did what good teams do against limited opponents: they controlled the game, protected Alisson, and conserved energy for the rounds that matter. A 3-0 that never threatened to be anything else.

There is a version of this Brazil side that dazzles, and we did not see it here. What we saw instead was efficiency, the quiet superpower Ancelotti has been trying to instil in a squad that has too often flattered to deceive at World Cups. Scotland competed in spells, pressed with honest intent, and were simply outclassed by a team that took its chances and declined to overexert. If you are Brazil, that is close to the ideal group-stage performance: three points, a clean sheet, minimal mileage on the key legs.

The obvious question is whether efficiency is enough when the opposition is not Scotland. Against France or Spain, Brazil will need the electricity as well as the control, and this performance, competent rather than commanding, did not fully answer that. But it did what it needed to, and it hinted at gears held in reserve. Vinícius looked sharp without hitting top speed; the attack functioned without being fully unleashed.

## What it means

That, ultimately, is the story of Brazil's tournament to this point: winning without spending everything, banking results while the flourishes wait. Whether that is a champion pacing itself or a team yet to find its ceiling is the debate that will define their summer. On this evidence, Ancelotti will not much mind which. Scotland go away having met a superior side; Brazil move on, unhurried, with plenty still in the tank.
