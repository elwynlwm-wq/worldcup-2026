On the FIFA ranking Canada sit 32, and the Elo model has them at 1764, which frames them as a dangerous outsider. Jesse Marsch's side advanced as group runners-up, winning 2, drawing 1 and losing 1 of 4 games while scoring 9 and conceding 3. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

This is a front-foot side that both makes chances and denies them, the profile of a team built to go deep. Across the games tracked they averaged 2.20 expected goals for and 0.60 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they drew 1-1 against Bosnia and Herzegovina, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (1.2 to 1.0 on xG). An even game that an even result did justice to. In their second group game they won 6-0 against Qatar, and they carried the greater threat, 4.6 to 0.2 on xG. A statement result that left little room for argument. In their third group game they lost 1-2 against Switzerland, and it stayed tight on xG, 1.7 to 1.1. They deserved more than nothing, which will sting all the more for it. In the round-of-32 tie they won 1-0 against South Africa, and the expected-goals split (1.3 to 0.1) says they made the better chances. A win earned rather than gifted, and taken with the composure the occasion asked for.

In attack the output matched the process, 9 goals from around 8.8 expected, which is the healthiest place to be: winning on repeatable chance-creation rather than hot streaks. Their most threatening display came against Qatar, worth 4.6 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Jonathan David (39), Cyle Larin (30), Alphonso Davies (15), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

Defensively they were miserly, conceding 3 and allowing only about 2.4 expected goals in the tracked games. A back line that gives up so little turns knockout football into a coin toss it can win from a single set piece. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Switzerland, who worked 1.1 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 2 clean sheets from 4 games.

The group-stage goals were spread around, Jonathan David (3) heading a list that also features Cyle Larin (2), Stephan Eustáquio (1), Promise David (1). A side scoring from several places is a harder side to plan against.

Individually, Nathan-Dylan Saliba has been the driver, 1 goal and 2 assists in 4 appearances at an average match rating of 7.5. Sides that lean on one player peak when he does and dip when he is quiet, and that dependence is worth watching in a knockout.

The team news matters too. Availability doubts hang over I. Koné (fracture of lower leg), A. Jones (muscle bruise), and how many of those clear up before kickoff shapes what this side can actually put on the pitch.

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers back the results: they created meaningfully more than they allowed, and that gap is usually the difference between a good run and an early flight home. A goal difference of +6 from 4 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

Set against the other 47 teams in the field, their attack ranks 6th for expected goals created and 5th for goals scored, while the defence ranks 5th for expected goals allowed. That is the truer placing, stripped of who they happened to draw, and it is the number to hold onto when the bracket starts throwing up mismatches and upsets.
