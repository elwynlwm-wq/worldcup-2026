Canada have never had a World Cup like this, and they have earned every minute of it. The question in Houston is not whether the ride has been remarkable. It is whether it runs into the one kind of team designed to end exactly this sort of story.

The numbers are unusual for a home nation: our model favours Morocco at 50% to Canada's 30, and the market is shorter still, roughly 54% Morocco. Sit with that. A co-host, riding a home crowd, on the best run in its history, is the underdog in its own Round of 16. That is not disrespect to Canada. It is respect for Morocco.

Jesse Marsch's side have been magnificent and, tellingly, resilient. A 6-0 demolition of Qatar, complete with a Jonathan David hat-trick, gave them their first ever World Cup win; a 1-0 defeat of South Africa in the Round of 32, settled by Stephen Eustáquio in the 92nd minute, gave them their first ever World Cup knockout win. Twice now they have decided games in stoppage time, which speaks to fitness and nerve, and David has looked every inch a top-line number nine. For a nation that had won a single World Cup match in its history before this summer, this is already a generational leap.

But Morocco are a different order of opponent, and it is worth being precise about why. This is not the group-stage class Canada out-muscled. Under Mohamed Ouahbi, who took over after Walid Regragui resigned in March following the AFCON final, Morocco have carried the spine of the 2022 semi-finalists into a high-pressing, technically superb 4-3-3. Achraf Hakimi overlaps for fun, Sofyan Amrabat shields the back four, and Brahim Díaz, fresh off a starring AFCON, provides the guile. And they have just done the thing they do best: knocked a European giant out on penalties, drawing 1-1 with the Netherlands before Yassine Bounou turned shootout hero, inflicting the Dutch's earliest ever World Cup exit. Echoes of 2022 are everywhere.

The head-to-head only deepens the concern. Morocco have never lost to Canada, including a 2-1 win at the 2022 World Cup, and stylistically they match up badly for Marsch's men. Canada's threat is transition, David running the channels with Cyle Larin, and stoppage-time belief. Morocco defend those things better than almost anyone left, they are comfortable making a game slow and territorial, and they have the shootout pedigree to survive a night where the goals do not come. There is also a fitness cloud that could tilt everything: Alphonso Davies, Canada's single biggest matchup problem, has been managing a hamstring, and his sharpness is the swing factor Marsch cannot fully control.

So we lean Morocco, and we lean towards it being tight, low-scoring and quite possibly settled from twelve yards, which is precisely the sort of night Morocco have made their own. Canada's best hope is an early goal that forces Morocco to chase and opens the counters David and Davies thrive on. Their worst-case is the game Morocco want: patient, physical, goalless deep into the second half, drifting towards penalties against the team that just eliminated the Netherlands that way.

The forward look sharpens the stakes. The winner goes to a quarter-final at Gillette Stadium against France or Paraguay, and that half of the draw has already turned strange: France eased past Sweden 3-0, but Paraguay knocked Germany out on penalties, so the giant is not guaranteed. For Canada, reaching a World Cup quarter-final on home soil would be the greatest day in the country's football history and would put Marsch, passed over for the United States job, in front of the very audience that doubted him. For Morocco, it is simply the next step of a project that has already changed what an African or Arab side is allowed to dream about, now under a new coach who has picked up the thread without dropping it.

If Canada are to spring it, the route is clear enough to sketch. They need the game fast and open rather than slow and territorial, and that means scoring first to force Morocco out of their patient shape and into the transitions where David and, if fit, Davies are lethal. A raucous Houston crowd helps, and Morocco, for all their control, can be got at when opponents commit numbers forward. The problem is that everything about Ouahbi's side is built to deny exactly that game, and their nerve from twelve yards, freshly proven against the Dutch, means Canada cannot even count on a shootout as the great leveller.

Our verdict: Morocco, narrowly, and probably the hard way. Canada's summer has been a triumph regardless of what happens on Saturday. But triumphs meet their level eventually, and Morocco's machine looks a level too far.
