## How it unfolded

Every so often a result does more than move a team up a table. Canada's 6-0 demolition of Qatar was their first ever World Cup win, and the manner of it, ruthless, front-footed, relentless, reframed how the rest of the tournament should think about the co-hosts.

Cyle Larin set the tone inside 16 minutes, but the afternoon belonged to Jonathan David. His hat-trick, two either side of the interval and a third in stoppage time, was the performance of a genuine number nine finding his stage, and it settled the question that had followed Canada into the tournament: do they have someone to finish the chances their pace and energy create? Emphatically, yes. Nathan Saliba added a fourth, an own goal made it five, and David completed his treble to round off a rout.

The honest caveat is that Qatar were poor, disorganised at the back and offering almost nothing going forward, and a scoreline like this flatters the winner as much as it condemns the loser. Six goals against a shambolic opponent is not proof that Canada can live with France or Brazil. But you can only beat what is in front of you, and few teams put six past anyone. What impressed was not the total so much as the attitude: Canada kept their foot down, kept moving the ball with purpose, and treated a mismatch as a chance to build belief rather than coast.

That belief is the real takeaway. A nation that had never won a World Cup match now had a statement to carry forward, and the confidence from an afternoon like this does not evaporate. It is the platform on which the rest of their historic run, the stoppage-time wins, the first knockout victory, would be built.

## What it means

Qatar, by contrast, were left with questions that go beyond one bad day. But the story here was all Canada: a country discovering, in real time and on home soil, that it belonged. David had the goals, Larin had the swagger, and a tournament had a new team worth watching.
