On the FIFA ranking Colombia sit 11, and the Elo model has them at 2004, which frames them as a genuine contender. Néstor Lorenzo's side topped their group, winning 2, drawing 1 and losing 0 of 3 games while scoring 4 and conceding 1. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

Their identity is control and containment, low-event games decided by the odd moment they are set up to provide. Across the games tracked they averaged 1.41 expected goals for and 0.74 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they win 3-1 against Uzbekistan, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (1.6 to 1.2 on xG). A win earned rather than gifted, and taken with the composure the occasion asked for. In their second group game they win 1-0 against DR Congo, and the expected-goals split (1.0 to 0.4) says they made the better chances. Control when it mattered, and enough at the other end to see it through. In their third group game they draw 0-0 against Portugal, and the expected-goals split (1.6 to 0.7) says they made the better chances. Two points dropped more than one earned, on the balance of the chances.

In attack the output matched the process, 4 goals from around 4.2 expected, which is the healthiest place to be: winning on repeatable chance-creation rather than hot streaks. Their most threatening display came against Portugal, worth 1.6 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with James Rodríguez (29), Luis Díaz (21), Yerry Mina (7), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

Defensively they were miserly, conceding 1 and allowing only about 2.2 expected goals in the tracked games. A back line that gives up so little turns knockout football into a coin toss it can win from a single set piece. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Uzbekistan, who worked 1.2 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 2 clean sheets from 3 games.

In this tournament the scoring has leaned on Daniel Muñoz, whose 2 make up over half of the group-stage goals. That is production and dependency in one number: mark him out and the supply can dry up.

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers sit close to even, the mark of a side that has to fight for every result rather than one that overwhelms. A goal difference of +3 from 6 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

They are into the knockouts now, where the 2004 rating and the xG edge matter less than holding a lead for ninety tense minutes. On this evidence they have the tools; whether they have the nerve is the one thing no model prices.
