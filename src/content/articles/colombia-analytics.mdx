On the FIFA ranking Colombia sit 11, and the Elo model has them at 2004, which frames them as a genuine contender. Néstor Lorenzo's side topped their group, winning 3, drawing 2 and losing 0 of 5 games while scoring 5 and conceding 1. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

## How they play

Their identity is control and containment, low-event games decided by the odd moment they are set up to provide. Across the games tracked they averaged 1.60 expected goals for and 0.62 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

## The campaign, game by game

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they won 3-1 against Uzbekistan, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (1.6 to 1.2 on xG). A win earned rather than gifted, and taken with the composure the occasion asked for. In their second group game they won 1-0 against DR Congo, and they carried the greater threat, 1.0 to 0.4 on xG. Control when it mattered, and enough at the other end to see it through. In their third group game they drew 0-0 against Portugal, and the openings fell their way, 1.6 expected goals to 0.7. Two points dropped more than one earned, on the balance of the chances. In the round-of-32 tie they beat Ghana 1-0, and the expected-goals split (2.2 to 0.3) says they made the better chances. A win earned rather than gifted, and taken with the composure the occasion asked for. In the round-of-16 tie they were held to a 0-0 draw by Switzerland and went out on penalties; nothing separated the sides across 120 minutes, and the shootout fell the other way.

## Attack

In attack the output matched the process, 5 goals from around 6.4 expected, which is the healthiest place to be: winning on repeatable chance-creation rather than hot streaks. Their most threatening display came against Ghana, worth 2.2 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with James Rodríguez (29), Luis Díaz (21), Yerry Mina (7), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

## Defence

Defensively they were miserly, conceding 1 and allowing only about 2.5 expected goals in the tracked games. A back line that gives up so little turns knockout football into a coin toss it can win from a single set piece. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Uzbekistan, who worked 1.2 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 4 clean sheets from 5 games.

In this tournament the scoring has leaned on Daniel Muñoz, whose 2 make up over half of the group-stage goals. That is production and dependency in one number: mark him out and the supply can dry up.

## The key men

Individually, Daniel Muñoz has been the driver, 2 goals in 3 appearances at an average match rating of 7.43. Sides that lean on one player peak when he does and dip when he is quiet, and that dependence is worth watching in a knockout.

## What the numbers say

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers sit close to even, the mark of a side that has to fight for every result rather than one that overwhelms. A goal difference of +4 from 6 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

That is the truer placing, stripped of who they happened to draw, and it is the number to hold onto when the bracket starts throwing up mismatches and upsets.

## The reckoning

Their tournament is over. It ended in the last 16, a shootout exit after a 0-0 draw to Switzerland on 7 July. There was nothing to separate the sides across 120 minutes; penalties decided it. The profile above is the record they leave behind, and the underlying numbers say they were closer to the level than the exit alone suggests.
