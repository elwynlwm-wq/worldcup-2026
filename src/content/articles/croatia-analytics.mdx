Croatia came into the 2026 World Cup as a genuine contender, seeded 13 in the world and rated 1905 on Elo. Zlatko Dalić's side reached the knockout rounds, winning 2, drawing 0 and losing 1 of 3 games while scoring 5 and conceding 5. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

They sit in the middle of the spectrum, neither dominant nor fragile, a side that has to earn everything it gets. Across the games tracked they averaged 0.94 expected goals for and 1.49 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they lost 2-4 against England, and the expected goals (0.7 to 3.2) had them second best on the run of play. No arguments about the result; they were beaten by the better side on the day. In their second group game they won 1-0 against Panama, and they carried the greater threat, 1.6 to 0.6 on xG. Control when it mattered, and enough at the other end to see it through. In their third group game they won 2-1 against Ghana, and it stayed tight on xG, 0.5 to 0.7. The scoreline was kinder than the performance, and they will know it.

In attack the finishing ran ahead of the process: 5 goals from roughly 2.8 expected. That is a fine line to walk, because clinical runs tend to regress, and a side relying on beating its xG is a side living slightly on credit. Their most threatening display came against Panama, worth 1.6 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Ivan Perišić (38), Andrej Kramarić (36), Luka Modrić (28), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

Defensively they were steady without being watertight, conceding 5 from around 4.5 expected. Solid enough to build on, not so solid that they can switch off. The heaviest pressure they faced came from England, who worked 3.2 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 1 clean sheet from 3 games.

The group-stage goals were spread around, Petar Sucic (1) heading a list that also features Petar Musa (1), Nikola Vlasic (1), Martin Baturina (1). A side scoring from several places is a harder side to plan against.

Individually, Petar Sučić has been the driver, 1 goal and 1 assist in 3 appearances at an average match rating of 7.2. Sides that lean on one player peak when he does and dip when he is quiet, and that dependence is worth watching in a knockout.

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers sit close to even, the mark of a side that has to fight for every result rather than one that overwhelms. A goal difference of +0 from 3 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

Set against the other 47 teams in the field, their attack ranks 29th for expected goals created and 19th for goals scored, while the defence ranks 34th for expected goals allowed. That is the truer placing, stripped of who they happened to draw, and it is the number to hold onto when the bracket starts throwing up mismatches and upsets.

Next comes Portugal in the round of 32 on 2 July.
