DR Congo came into the 2026 World Cup as a dangerous outsider, seeded 46 in the world and rated 1712 on Elo. Sébastien Desabre's side reached the knockout rounds, winning 1, drawing 1 and losing 1 of 3 games while scoring 4 and conceding 3. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

Their identity is control and containment, low-event games decided by the odd moment they are set up to provide. Across the games tracked they averaged 1.20 expected goals for and 0.63 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they draw 1-1 against Portugal, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (0.9 to 0.7 on xG). An even game that an even result did justice to. In their second group game they defeat 0-1 against Colombia, and the expected goals (0.4 to 1.0) suggest they were second best on the run of play. No arguments about the result; they were beaten by the better side on the day. In their third group game they win 3-1 against Uzbekistan, and the expected-goals split (2.4 to 0.3) says they made the better chances. Not spectacular, but exactly the sort of result good sides grind out.

In attack the output matched the process, 4 goals from around 3.6 expected, which is the healthiest place to be: winning on repeatable chance-creation rather than hot streaks. Their most threatening display came against Uzbekistan, worth 2.4 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Cédric Bakambu (21), Meschak Elia (12), Yoane Wissa (9), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

Defensively they were miserly, conceding 3 and allowing only about 1.9 expected goals in the tracked games. A back line that gives up so little turns knockout football into a coin toss it can win from a single set piece. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Colombia, who worked 1.0 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 0 clean sheets from 3 games.

In this tournament the scoring has leaned on Yoane Wissa, whose 3 make up over half of the group-stage goals. That is production and dependency in one number: mark him out and the supply can dry up.

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers sit close to even, the mark of a side that has to fight for every result rather than one that overwhelms. A goal difference of +1 from 1 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

They are into the knockouts now, where the 1712 rating and the xG edge matter less than holding a lead for ninety tense minutes. On this evidence they have the tools; whether they have the nerve is the one thing no model prices.
