England came into the 2026 World Cup as one of the tournament favourites, seeded 4 in the world and rated 2038 on Elo. Thomas Tuchel's side reached the knockout rounds, winning 2, drawing 1 and losing 0 of 3 games while scoring 6 and conceding 2. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

This is a front-foot side that both makes chances and denies them, the profile of a team built to go deep. Across the games tracked they averaged 2.02 expected goals for and 0.48 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they win 4-2 against Croatia, and the expected-goals split (3.2 to 0.7) says they made the better chances. A win earned rather than gifted, and taken with the composure the occasion asked for. In their second group game they draw 0-0 against Ghana, and the expected-goals split (1.4 to 0.2) says they made the better chances. Two points dropped more than one earned, on the balance of the chances. In their third group game they win 2-0 against Panama, and the expected-goals split (1.5 to 0.6) says they made the better chances. Not spectacular, but exactly the sort of result good sides grind out.

In attack the output matched the process, 6 goals from around 6.1 expected, which is the healthiest place to be: winning on repeatable chance-creation rather than hot streaks. Their most threatening display came against Croatia, worth 3.2 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Harry Kane (78), Marcus Rashford (19), Bukayo Saka (14), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

Defensively they were miserly, conceding 2 and allowing only about 1.4 expected goals in the tracked games. A back line that gives up so little turns knockout football into a coin toss it can win from a single set piece. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Croatia, who worked 0.7 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 2 clean sheets from 3 games.

In this tournament the scoring has leaned on Harry Kane, whose 3 make up over half of the group-stage goals. That is production and dependency in one number: mark him out and the supply can dry up.

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers back the results: they created meaningfully more than they allowed, and that gap is usually the difference between a good run and an early flight home. A goal difference of +4 from 4 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

They are into the knockouts now, where the 2038 rating and the xG edge matter less than holding a lead for ninety tense minutes. On this evidence they have the tools; whether they have the nerve is the one thing no model prices.
