## How it unfolded

While the favourites around them wobbled, France did the one thing the model and the market both demanded: they made a good team look ordinary.

Our forecast had France as heavy favourites, the tournament's top-rated side, and this was the performance of a team that knows it. Kylian Mbappé's opener on the stroke of half-time took the sting out of a stubborn Sweden, Bradley Barcola made it two within eight minutes of the restart, and from there it was an exhibition. Mbappé's second, a familiar blur of pace and a cold finish, put the result beyond doubt and the tie into cruise control long before the hour was up.

What should worry the rest is not that France won, but how little it cost them. There was no scare, no shootout, no late drama, just control, then a burst of quality, then game management. On a weekend when Germany went out, the Netherlands were dumped, and Brazil needed the last kick to survive, France strolled. That contrast is the whole point. The teams around them are grinding; France are conserving.

Sweden were organised and game, but they were beaten by the gap in individual quality, and against Mbappé in this mood there is not much a defence can do. Once France go two up with time to spare, they are close to unplayable, precisely because they no longer need to take risks.

## What it means

France march into the last 16 as the clear team to beat, and the draw is opening in their favour: Germany gone, several of their rivals dragged through the wringer. If there is a caution, it is the oldest one in tournament football, that a smooth ride can leave a side under-tested when the real examination comes. But that is a problem for the quarter-finals. On this evidence, France look like the side everyone else is trying to avoid, and with good reason.
