Rated 1916 on Elo and 12 in the world, Germany arrived as a genuine contender. Julian Nagelsmann's side topped their group, winning 2, drawing 1 and losing 1 of 4 games while scoring 11 and conceding 5. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

## How they play

This is a front-foot side that both makes chances and denies them, the profile of a team built to go deep. Across the games tracked they averaged 2.25 expected goals for and 0.97 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

## The campaign, game by game

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they won 7-1 against Curacao, and the expected-goals split (4.2 to 0.4) says they made the better chances. It was the kind of afternoon where everything they tried came off. In their second group game they won 2-1 against Ivory Coast, and they carried the greater threat, 1.9 to 1.2 on xG. Control when it mattered, and enough at the other end to see it through. In their third group game they lost 1-2 against Ecuador, and the better chances went the other way, 0.7 to 1.3 on expected goals. No arguments about the result; they were beaten by the better side on the day. In the round-of-32 tie they drew 1-1 with Paraguay before penalties settled it. There was nothing to separate the sides in normal time, and the shootout is where models hand over to nerve.

## Attack

In attack the finishing ran ahead of the process: 11 goals from roughly 6.8 expected. That is a fine line to walk, because clinical runs tend to regress, and a side relying on beating its xG is a side living slightly on credit. Their most threatening display came against Curacao, worth 4.2 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Kai Havertz (21), Leroy Sané (16), Leon Goretzka (15), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

## Defence

Defensively they were steady without being watertight, conceding 5 from around 2.9 expected. Solid enough to build on, not so solid that they can switch off. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Ecuador, who worked 1.3 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 0 clean sheets from 4 games.

The group-stage goals were spread around, Deniz Undav (3) heading a list that also features Kai Havertz (2), Nico Schlotterbeck (1), Nathaniel Brown (1). A side scoring from several places is a harder side to plan against.

## The key men

Individually, Deniz Undav has been the driver, 3 goals and 2 assists in 3 appearances at an average match rating of 7.63. Sides that lean on one player peak when he does and dip when he is quiet, and that dependence is worth watching in a knockout.

The team news matters too. Availability doubts hang over N. Brown (adductor injury), N. Schlotterbeck (ligament stretching), and how many of those clear up before kickoff shapes what this side can actually put on the pitch.

## What the numbers say

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers back the results: they created meaningfully more than they allowed, and that gap is usually the difference between a good run and an early flight home. A goal difference of +6 from 6 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

Set against the other 47 teams in the field, their attack ranks 2nd for expected goals created and 2nd for goals scored, while the defence ranks 21st for expected goals allowed. That is the truer placing, stripped of who they happened to draw, and it is the number to hold onto when the bracket starts throwing up mismatches and upsets.

## What's next

They are into the knockouts, where the 1916 rating and the xG edge matter less than holding a lead for ninety tense minutes.
