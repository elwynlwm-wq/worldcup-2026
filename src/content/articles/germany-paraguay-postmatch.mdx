This is the result that blows a hole in the bracket. Germany, one of the pre-tournament contenders and a comfortable favourite on our model, are out, and Paraguay, organised and fearless, are the team nobody wanted to draw and now the team nobody can avoid.

The warning signs were there in the group. Germany looked heavy, a little slow, a side trading on reputation more than rhythm, and Paraguay are precisely the kind of opponent built to punish that. Julio Enciso's opener just before half-time was the reward for a game plan executed to the letter: sit compact, deny space, break with intent. Kai Havertz's equaliser after the interval settled German nerves and, on the balance of talent, should have been the moment the favourites took over.

They never did. Paraguay defended the way they have all tournament, in two banks, bodies in every lane, goalkeeper commanding his box, and dragged Germany exactly where a limited side wants a great one: to penalties, where reputation counts for nothing. Twelve yards later, Paraguay were through and Germany were reaching for the same tired questions that have followed them out of tournament after tournament.

For Germany this is a genuine crisis, another early exit for a football nation that has spent a decade insisting it is about to turn the corner. For Paraguay it is the run of a lifetime, and it is not luck. A team that draws matches, frustrates favourites and backs itself in shootouts is a team with a method, and methods travel in knockout football.

Paraguay march into the last 16 with a real spring in their step and a draw that has just opened up around them. Beware the side that beats a giant on penalties: they arrive at the next round with nothing to fear and a nation suddenly believing. Germany go home to ask, again, what exactly they are for.
