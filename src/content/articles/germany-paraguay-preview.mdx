On the numbers this looks straightforward. The market makes Germany clear favourites at around 70%, and our model agrees on the direction if not the size, closer to 60%. Germany have the deeper, more talented squad, and on paper they should have too much for Paraguay. The question is whether paper has been telling the truth about this Germany side.

Because the doubts are real. Germany arrived at the tournament looking heavy and a little slow, a team leaning on reputation more than rhythm, and that is precisely the profile Paraguay are built to punish. Gustavo Alfaro's side do the unglamorous things well: they sit compact, deny space, and break with intent through Julio Enciso and Miguel Almirón. They do not beat themselves, and they have made a habit of dragging better teams into the kind of low-scoring, nervy contest where favourites get anxious.

Germany's route through is clear enough, control the ball, use their quality in the final third, and avoid letting the game become a scrap. If Havertz and the German attack click early, this is comfortable. The danger is the alternative script: a tight, tense game still level with twenty minutes to go, a crowd getting restless, and a Paraguay side that grows in belief the longer it stays deadlocked.

## By the numbers

Our lean is Germany, but with far less confidence than the odds suggest, and we would not rule out this going to extra time or even penalties, where Paraguay's discipline and nerve make them awkward opponents. Beware the stubborn underdog who only needs one moment; Paraguay are exactly that.

The head-to-head is thin, two inconclusive meetings and a 3-3 the last time in 2013. Germany carry knocks to Nico Schlotterbeck and Nathaniel Brown, but the gap in group-stage expected goals, comfortably over two a game to Paraguay's fraction of that, is the number that should decide it.

## The call

Prediction: Germany to edge it, but do not be surprised if they are made to sweat every minute of it.
