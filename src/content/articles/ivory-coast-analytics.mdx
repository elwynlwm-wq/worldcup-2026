Rated 1743 on Elo and 30 in the world, Ivory Coast arrived as a seeded side with real quality. Emerse Faé's side advanced as group runners-up, winning 2, drawing 0 and losing 2 of 4 games while scoring 5 and conceding 4. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

## How they play

They sit in the middle of the spectrum, neither dominant nor fragile, a side that has to earn everything it gets. Across the games tracked they averaged 1.35 expected goals for and 1.35 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

## The campaign, game by game

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they won 1-0 against Ecuador, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (1.5 to 1.0 on xG). A win earned rather than gifted, and taken with the composure the occasion asked for. In their second group game they lost 1-2 against Germany, and they were out-created, 1.2 to 1.9 on xG. ## Attack

In attack the output matched the process, 5 goals from around 5.4 expected, which is the healthiest place to be: winning on repeatable chance-creation rather than hot streaks. Their most threatening display came against Ecuador, worth 1.5 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Franck Kessié (15), Ibrahim Sangaré (12), Nicolas Pépé (12), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

## Defence

Defensively they were steady without being watertight, conceding 4 from around 5.4 expected. Solid enough to build on, not so solid that they can switch off. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Norway, who worked 2.0 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 2 clean sheets from 4 games.

In this tournament the scoring has leaned on Nicolas Pépé, whose 2 make up over half of the group-stage goals. That is production and dependency in one number: mark him out and the supply can dry up.

## The key men

Individually, Nicolas Pépé has been the driver, 2 goals and 1 assist in 4 appearances at an average match rating of 7.1. Sides that lean on one player peak when he does and dip when he is quiet, and that dependence is worth watching in a knockout.

## What the numbers say

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers sit close to even, the mark of a side that has to fight for every result rather than one that overwhelms. A goal difference of +1 from 6 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

Set against the other 47 teams in the field, their attack ranks 20th for expected goals created and 19th for goals scored, while the defence ranks 28th for expected goals allowed. That is the truer placing, stripped of who they happened to draw, and it is the number to hold onto when the bracket starts throwing up mismatches and upsets.
