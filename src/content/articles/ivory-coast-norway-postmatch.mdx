Our model made Norway favourites, and in the end the reason why walked the ball into the net with four minutes to play. When you have Erling Haaland, tight games have a habit of tilting your way.

For an hour this was a proper contest. Antonio Nusa gave Norway a deserved lead just before half-time, the reward for a side that carried the greater threat in transition, but Ivory Coast are no soft touch, and Amad Diallo's 74th-minute equaliser was earned. At 1-1 with a quarter of an hour left, the tie was genuinely up for grabs, and the Ivorians, roared on, looked the more likely to snatch it.

Then Haaland. Norway's whole tournament has been built around the simple, brutal truth that they possess the most reliable finisher in world football, and in the 86th minute he settled it, the striker arriving exactly where strikers are supposed to arrive to break Ivorian hearts. It was not a classic Norway performance, they rode their luck in spells and were second best for a stretch, but they had the one player who turns a coin-flip into a result.

That is both the strength and the question mark. Norway are dangerous because Haaland makes them dangerous, and a team that leans this heavily on one man is a team that lives and dies by his afternoon. Against the better sides still in the draw, they will need more than moments; they will need to control games rather than survive them. But you do not argue with a striker in this form, and while he keeps scoring the winners, Norway will keep advancing.

Ivory Coast can feel aggrieved. They matched a fancied opponent for long stretches and were undone by a single piece of world-class quality, which is the cruel arithmetic of knockout football. Norway go through, Haaland's legend grows, and the rest of the tournament has been reminded, again, that the surest way to lose a tight game is to leave the Norwegian even half a yard.
