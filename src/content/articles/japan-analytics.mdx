Japan came into the 2026 World Cup as a seeded side with real quality, seeded 17 in the world and rated 1910 on Elo. Hajime Moriyasu's side advanced as group runners-up, winning 1, drawing 2 and losing 1 of 4 games while scoring 8 and conceding 5. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

Their identity is control and containment, low-event games decided by the odd moment they are set up to provide. Across the games tracked they averaged 1.04 expected goals for and 0.79 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they drew 2-2 against Netherlands, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (0.6 to 0.8 on xG). An even game that an even result did justice to. In their second group game they won 4-0 against Tunisia, and they carried the greater threat, 2.1 to 0.1 on xG. A statement result that left little room for argument. In their third group game they drew 1-1 against Sweden, and it stayed tight on xG, 1.2 to 0.6. Two points dropped more than one earned, on the balance of the chances. In the round-of-32 tie they lost 1-2 against Brazil, and the expected goals (0.2 to 1.7) had them second best on the run of play. No arguments about the result; they were beaten by the better side on the day.

In attack the finishing ran ahead of the process: 8 goals from roughly 4.2 expected. That is a fine line to walk, because clinical runs tend to regress, and a side relying on beating its xG is a side living slightly on credit. Their most threatening display came against Tunisia, worth 2.1 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Ayase Ueda (16), Junya Itō (15), Daichi Kamada (12), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

Defensively they were miserly, conceding 5 and allowing only about 3.2 expected goals in the tracked games. A back line that gives up so little turns knockout football into a coin toss it can win from a single set piece. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Brazil, who worked 1.7 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 1 clean sheet from 4 games.

The group-stage goals were spread around, Daichi Kamada (2) heading a list that also features Ayase Ueda (2), Keito Nakamura (1), Junya Ito (1). A side scoring from several places is a harder side to plan against.

Individually, Ayase Ueda has been the driver, 2 goals and 1 assist in 4 appearances at an average match rating of 7.28. Sides that lean on one player peak when he does and dip when he is quiet, and that dependence is worth watching in a knockout.

The team news matters too. Availability doubts hang over S. Machino (illness), T. Kubo (injury), and how many of those clear up before kickoff shapes what this side can actually put on the pitch.

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers sit close to even, the mark of a side that has to fight for every result rather than one that overwhelms. A goal difference of +3 from 5 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

Set against the other 47 teams in the field, their attack ranks 28th for expected goals created and 7th for goals scored, while the defence ranks 11th for expected goals allowed. That is the truer placing, stripped of who they happened to draw, and it is the number to hold onto when the bracket starts throwing up mismatches and upsets.

They are into the knockouts, where the 1910 rating and the xG edge matter less than holding a lead for ninety tense minutes.
