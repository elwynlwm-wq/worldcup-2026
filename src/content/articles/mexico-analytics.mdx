Rated 1912 on Elo and 9 in the world, Mexico arrived as a genuine contender. Javier Aguirre's side topped their group, winning 4, drawing 0 and losing 1 of 5 games while scoring 10 and conceding 3. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

## How they play

Their identity is control and containment, low-event games decided by the odd moment they are set up to provide. Across the games tracked they averaged 1.33 expected goals for and 0.75 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

## The campaign, game by game

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they won 2-0 against South Africa, and the expected-goals split (1.5 to 0.1) says they made the better chances. A win earned rather than gifted, and taken with the composure the occasion asked for. In their second group game they won 1-0 against South Korea, and neither side made much, 0.5 to 0.9 on the underlying numbers. The scoreline was kinder than the performance, and they will know it. In their third group game they won 3-0 against Czechia, and the openings fell their way, 1.8 expected goals to 0.5. The margin flattered no one; they were that far ahead. In the round-of-32 tie they won 2-0 against Ecuador, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (1.0 to 0.7 on xG). A win earned rather than gifted, and taken with the composure the occasion asked for. In the round-of-16 tie they lost 2-3 to England, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (1.9 to 1.6 on xG). They made the better chances and still went home, which is the cruelty of a single knockout tie.

## Attack

In attack the finishing ran ahead of the process: 10 goals from roughly 6.7 expected. That is a fine line to walk, because clinical runs tend to regress, and a side relying on beating its xG is a side living slightly on credit. Their most threatening display came against England, worth 1.9 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Raúl Jiménez (44), Orbelín Pineda (12), Edson Álvarez (7), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

## Defence

Defensively they were miserly, conceding 3 and allowing only about 3.7 expected goals in the tracked games. A back line that gives up so little turns knockout football into a coin toss it can win from a single set piece. The heaviest pressure they faced came from England, who worked 1.6 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 4 clean sheets from 5 games.

The group-stage goals were spread around, Julián Quiñones (2) heading a list that also features Álvaro Fidalgo (1), Raúl Jiménez (1), Mateo Chávez (1). A side scoring from several places is a harder side to plan against.

## The key men

Individually, Julián Quiñones has been the driver, 3 goals and 1 assist in 4 appearances at an average match rating of 7.72. Sides that lean on one player peak when he does and dip when he is quiet, and that dependence is worth watching in a knockout.

## What the numbers say

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers back the results: they created meaningfully more than they allowed, and that gap is usually the difference between a good run and an early flight home. A goal difference of +7 from 9 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

Set against the other 47 teams in the field, their attack ranks 26th for expected goals created and 7th for goals scored, while the defence ranks 4th for expected goals allowed. That is the truer placing, stripped of who they happened to draw, and it is the number to hold onto when the bracket starts throwing up mismatches and upsets.

## The reckoning

Their tournament is over. It ended in the last 16, a 2-3 defeat to England on 6 July. On the expected-goals evidence they had the better of it and can feel hard done by. The profile above is the record they leave behind, and the underlying numbers say they were closer to the level than the exit alone suggests.
