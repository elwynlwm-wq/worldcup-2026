Rated 1877 on Elo and 6 in the world, Morocco arrived as one of the tournament favourites. Mohamed Ouahbi's side advanced as group runners-up, winning 2, drawing 2 and losing 0 of 4 games while scoring 7 and conceding 4. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

This is a front-foot side that both makes chances and denies them, the profile of a team built to go deep. Across the games tracked they averaged 2.04 expected goals for and 0.81 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they draw 1-1 against Brazil, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (1.4 to 1.3 on xG). An even game that an even result did justice to. In their second group game they win 1-0 against Scotland, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (1.0 to 0.5 on xG). Control when it mattered, and enough at the other end to see it through. In their third group game they win 4-2 against Haiti, and the expected-goals split (3.8 to 0.7) says they made the better chances. Not spectacular, but exactly the sort of result good sides grind out. In the round-of-32 tie they drew 1-1 with Netherlands before penalties settled it. There was nothing to separate the sides in normal time, and the shootout is where models hand over to nerve.

In attack the output matched the process, 7 goals from around 6.1 expected, which is the healthiest place to be: winning on repeatable chance-creation rather than hot streaks. Their most threatening display came against Haiti, worth 3.8 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Ayoub El Kaabi (22), Brahim Díaz (13), Achraf Hakimi (11), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

Defensively they were miserly, conceding 4 and allowing only about 2.4 expected goals in the tracked games. A back line that gives up so little turns knockout football into a coin toss it can win from a single set piece. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Brazil, who worked 1.3 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 1 clean sheet from 4 games.

In this tournament the scoring has leaned on Ismael Saibari, whose 3 make up over half of the group-stage goals. That is production and dependency in one number: mark him out and the supply can dry up.

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers back the results: they created meaningfully more than they allowed, and that gap is usually the difference between a good run and an early flight home. A goal difference of +3 from 7 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

They are into the knockouts now, where the 1877 rating and the xG edge matter less than holding a lead for ninety tense minutes. On this evidence they have the tools; whether they have the nerve is the one thing no model prices.
