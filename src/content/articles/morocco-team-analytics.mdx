Morocco are into the last 16 having already knocked out the Netherlands, and the instinct is to call it a shock. The data says it is a method.

Start with the road here. A 1-1 draw with Brazil in which the expected goals were dead level (1.37 for Morocco, 1.26 for Brazil) — not a smash-and-grab, a genuine toe-to-toe with the tournament favourites. A 1-0 win over Scotland (0.99 xG to 0.51). A 4-2 dismantling of Haiti built on a huge 3.76 xG. Then the Netherlands in the Round of 32: 1-1, and through on penalties, Yassine Bounou the shootout hero. Across four games, Morocco have not once been outplayed on chances, and they have taken points off a superpower, a European side and beaten a Dutch team many fancied.

The profile is clear. This is not a team that overwhelms opponents with volume; it is a team that stays compact, concedes almost nothing of quality, and backs its match-winners to settle things. Ranked sixth in the world and rated accordingly by our model, Morocco under Mohamed Ouahbi — note, not Walid Regragui, who left after the AFCON final — have kept the spine of the 2022 semi-finalists and added a ruthless edge in the big moments.

The individual data backs the eye test. Ayoub El Kaabi leads the line with 22 international goals, the kind of penalty-box finisher who punishes the one chance a low-scoring game offers. Around him, Brahim Díaz (13) provides the guile, Achraf Hakimi (11 goals from full-back, absurdly) the width and drive, and Soufiane Rahimi and Azzedine Ounahi the legs and creativity in midfield. It is a balanced, top-tier spine — no obvious weak link for opponents to target, and crucially, no injuries flagged going into the knockouts.

What the numbers really reveal is temperament. Morocco's whole game is designed for exactly the scenario that undoes better sides: a tight match, level deep into the second half, drifting towards penalties. They defend those situations better than almost anyone left, and their shootout pedigree, freshly proven against the Dutch and echoing 2022, means "level after 90" is a state they are comfortable in and their opponents dread.

That is the warning for Canada, who meet them next, and for everyone beyond. On raw talent, several teams left in the draw are Morocco's equal. On the specific skill of winning knockout football — staying in the fight, conceding nothing cheap, and holding your nerve when it goes the distance — few can match them. The giant-killing is not luck. It is the plan, and the data says the plan is working.
