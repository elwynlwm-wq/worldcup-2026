Rated 1918 on Elo and 23 in the world, Norway arrived as a seeded side with real quality. Stale Solbakken's side advanced as group runners-up, winning 3, drawing 0 and losing 1 of 4 games while scoring 10 and conceding 8. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

They are an attacking team first, happy to trade chances and back their forwards to win the exchange. Across the games tracked they averaged 2.11 expected goals for and 1.30 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they win 4-1 against Iraq, and the expected-goals split (2.5 to 0.8) says they made the better chances. It was the kind of afternoon where everything they tried came off. In their second group game they win 3-2 against Senegal, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (2.2 to 1.7 on xG). Control when it mattered, and enough at the other end to see it through. In their third group game they defeat 1-4 against France, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (1.7 to 1.3 on xG). They deserved more than nothing, which will sting all the more for it. In the round-of-32 tie they win 2-1 against Ivory Coast, and the expected-goals split (2.0 to 1.4) says they made the better chances. A win earned rather than gifted, and taken with the composure the occasion asked for.

In attack the finishing ran ahead of the process: 10 goals from roughly 8.4 expected. That is a fine line to walk, because clinical runs tend to regress, and a side relying on beating its xG is a side living slightly on credit. Their most threatening display came against Iraq, worth 2.5 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Erling Haaland (55), Alexander Sorloth (26), Antonio Nusa (8), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

Defensively they were steady without being watertight, conceding 8 from around 5.2 expected. Solid enough to build on, not so solid that they can switch off. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Senegal, who worked 1.7 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 0 clean sheets from 4 games.

In this tournament the scoring has leaned on Erling Haaland, whose 4 make up over half of the group-stage goals. That is production and dependency in one number: mark him out and the supply can dry up.

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers back the results: they created meaningfully more than they allowed, and that gap is usually the difference between a good run and an early flight home. A goal difference of +2 from 6 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

They are into the knockouts now, where the 1918 rating and the xG edge matter less than holding a lead for ninety tense minutes. On this evidence they have the tools; whether they have the nerve is the one thing no model prices.
