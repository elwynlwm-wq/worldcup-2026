On the FIFA ranking Portugal sit 8, and the Elo model has them at 1990, which frames them as a genuine contender. Roberto Martínez's side reached the knockout rounds, winning 1, drawing 2 and losing 0 of 3 games while scoring 6 and conceding 1. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

Their identity is control and containment, low-event games decided by the odd moment they are set up to provide. Across the games tracked they averaged 1.32 expected goals for and 0.91 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they draw 1-1 against DR Congo, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (0.7 to 0.9 on xG). An even game that an even result did justice to. In their second group game they win 5-0 against Uzbekistan, and the expected-goals split (2.6 to 0.2) says they made the better chances. A statement result that left little room for argument. In their third group game they draw 0-0 against Colombia, and the expected goals (0.7 to 1.6) suggest they were second best on the run of play. A point that owed plenty to riding their luck.

In attack the finishing ran ahead of the process: 6 goals from roughly 3.9 expected. That is a fine line to walk, because clinical runs tend to regress, and a side relying on beating its xG is a side living slightly on credit. Their most threatening display came against Uzbekistan, worth 2.6 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Cristiano Ronaldo (138), Bruno Fernandes (29), Bernardo Silva (13), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

Defensively they were steady without being watertight, conceding 1 from around 2.7 expected. Solid enough to build on, not so solid that they can switch off. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Colombia, who worked 1.6 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 2 clean sheets from 3 games.

The group-stage goals were spread around, Cristiano Ronaldo (2) heading a list that also features Rafael Leão (1), Nuno Mendes (1), João Neves (1). A side scoring from several places is a harder side to plan against.

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers sit close to even, the mark of a side that has to fight for every result rather than one that overwhelms. A goal difference of +5 from 4 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

They are into the knockouts now, where the 1990 rating and the xG edge matter less than holding a lead for ninety tense minutes. On this evidence they have the tools; whether they have the nerve is the one thing no model prices.
