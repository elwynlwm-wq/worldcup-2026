On the FIFA ranking Portugal sit 8, and the Elo model has them at 1990, which frames them as a genuine contender. Roberto Martínez's side reached the knockout rounds, winning 2, drawing 2 and losing 1 of 5 games while scoring 8 and conceding 3. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

## How they play

Their identity is control and containment, low-event games decided by the odd moment they are set up to provide. Across the games tracked they averaged 1.53 expected goals for and 1.02 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

## The campaign, game by game

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they drew 1-1 against DR Congo, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (0.7 to 0.9 on xG). An even game that an even result did justice to. In their second group game they won 5-0 against Uzbekistan, and they carried the greater threat, 2.6 to 0.2 on xG. A statement result that left little room for argument. In their third group game they drew 0-0 against Colombia, and the better chances went the other way, 0.7 to 1.6 on expected goals. A point that owed plenty to riding their luck. In the round-of-32 tie they beat Croatia 2-1, and the expected-goals split (2.2 to 1.3) says they made the better chances. A win earned rather than gifted, and taken with the composure the occasion asked for. In the round-of-16 tie they lost 0-1 to Spain. The better side went through, and the underlying numbers do not argue otherwise.

## Attack

In attack the finishing ran ahead of the process: 8 goals from roughly 6.1 expected. That is a fine line to walk, because clinical runs tend to regress, and a side relying on beating its xG is a side living slightly on credit. Their most threatening display came against Uzbekistan, worth 2.6 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Cristiano Ronaldo (138), Bruno Fernandes (29), Bernardo Silva (13), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

## Defence

Defensively they were steady without being watertight, conceding 3 from around 4.1 expected. Solid enough to build on, not so solid that they can switch off. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Colombia, who worked 1.6 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 2 clean sheets from 5 games.

The group-stage goals were spread around, Cristiano Ronaldo (2) heading a list that also features Rafael Leão (1), Nuno Mendes (1), João Neves (1). A side scoring from several places is a harder side to plan against.

## The key men

Individually, Cristiano Ronaldo has been the driver, 2 goals in 3 appearances at an average match rating of 7.07. Sides that lean on one player peak when he does and dip when he is quiet, and that dependence is worth watching in a knockout.

## What the numbers say

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers sit close to even, the mark of a side that has to fight for every result rather than one that overwhelms. A goal difference of +5 from 4 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

Set against the other 47 teams in the field, their attack ranks 21st for expected goals created and 15th for goals scored, while the defence ranks 19th for expected goals allowed. That is the truer placing, stripped of who they happened to draw, and it is the number to hold onto when the bracket starts throwing up mismatches and upsets.

## The reckoning

Their tournament is over. It ended in the last 16, a 0-1 defeat to Spain on 6 July. The margins told the story, and the better side went through. The profile above is the record they leave behind, and the underlying numbers say they were closer to the level than the exit alone suggests.
