Some ties you rank on talent. This one you rank on temperament, and that changes the answer.

On paper Portugal are the better team, comfortably. Roberto Martínez has the deepest squad left in the tournament: Vitinha and João Neves controlling midfield, Nuno Mendes flying down the left, Bruno Fernandes and Pedro Neto to unlock a block, and a 41-year-old Cristiano Ronaldo who still scored twice against Uzbekistan. Our model makes them 57% to win in 90 minutes and the market agrees at a shorter 53%. Everyone fancies Portugal. Nobody is confident. That gap between "favourites" and "trusted" is the whole story of their group.

Because Portugal arrived here blunt. A 1-1 draw with DR Congo and a 0-0 with Colombia bookended the Uzbekistan thrashing, and in the two games that required patience rather than a mismatch, they looked exactly like a side that runs its attack through one aging superstar and hopes. Five goals in one match flatters a group stage where they couldn't break down organised opponents. Croatia are nothing if not organised.

This is the trap. Zlatko Dalić's team are old, slow and, on the evidence of single-goal wins over Panama and Ghana, not much fun to watch. They are also the last side in the draw you want to meet when a tie is tight. Luka Modrić, at 40, still sets the tempo, and with Mateo Kovačić and the emerging Luka Sučić alongside him, Croatia will happily strangle the game, keep it goalless deep into the second half, and drag Portugal somewhere they hate to go. Croatia have made a decade-long career out of turning superior teams into anxious ones, and penalties are their natural habitat.

Which brings us to the number that should worry Portugal more than any rating. In six World Cups, across every knockout match he has ever played, Cristiano Ronaldo has never scored. Not once. It is the single hole in the greatest goalscoring record the game has produced, and it exists precisely because knockout football rewards the things Ronaldo's game has shed with age: pressing, movement off the ball, patience when the first chance does not come. Croatia are built to make him wait, and a man who has waited six tournaments does not suddenly solve it at 41.

So what actually happens? We think Portugal edge through, but not in the manner the odds imply. Expect a cagey, low-scoring night in Toronto, Croatia comfortable without the ball, Portugal probing and frustrated, the game still level past the hour. Portugal's bench is the difference: Martínez can change a stalemate in a way Dalić, with his veteran core, cannot. Our lean is a narrow Portugal win, and a real chance, higher than the 20% draw the market prices, that this one needs extra time.

There is a specific tactical reason to trust Croatia's stubbornness here. Portugal's best route to goal is width and overloads down the left through Nuno Mendes, but Dalić's sides defend the flanks in numbers and dare you to beat them centrally, where Modrić and Kovačić screen every pocket Bruno Fernandes wants to find. Take away the easy overload and Portugal are left crossing towards a 41-year-old target man, which is exactly the low-percentage football Croatia are content to defend all night.

It also asks a hard question of Martínez. His Portugal have talent in every position and keep producing flat, controlled performances that never catch fire, and a manager already under scrutiny at home cannot afford a cautious night that drifts to penalties against a team that lives for them. Set pieces may decide it, and there, at least, Croatia's aging bodies are a genuine vulnerability worth attacking early.

The forward look is where it gets interesting. The winner walks into a Round of 16 against whoever survives Spain versus Austria the same day, and that is a kinder draw than it sounds, because Spain have been just as unconvincing. Portugal's road to a semi-final is genuinely open. But that only matters if they get through a Croatia side that specialises in making sure the better team goes home wondering how. Back Portugal, hold your breath, and do not be shocked if Ronaldo's ghost is still unexorcised when the whistle goes.
