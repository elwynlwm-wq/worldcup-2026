On the FIFA ranking Scotland sit 41, and the Elo model has them at 1745, which frames them as a dangerous outsider. Steve Clarke's side reached the knockout rounds, winning 1, drawing 0 and losing 2 of 3 games while scoring 1 and conceding 4. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

## How they play

They sit in the middle of the spectrum, neither dominant nor fragile, a side that has to earn everything it gets. Across the games tracked they averaged 0.87 expected goals for and 2.12 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

## The campaign, game by game

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they won 1-0 against Haiti, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (1.1 to 1.1 on xG). A win earned rather than gifted, and taken with the composure the occasion asked for. In their second group game they lost 0-1 against Morocco, and neither side made much, 0.5 to 1.0 on the underlying numbers. A defeat that leaves questions without offering many answers. In their third group game they lost 0-3 against Brazil, and the better chances went the other way, 1.0 to 4.3 on expected goals. No arguments about the result; they were beaten by the better side on the day.

## Attack

In attack the chances outran the returns: about 2.6 expected goals for only 1 scored. The creation is there, and if the finishing catches up they are more dangerous than the goal tally suggests. Their most threatening display came against Haiti, worth 1.1 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with John McGinn (20), Scott McTominay (14), Ché Adams (11), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

## Defence

Defensively they were stretched, shipping 4 and giving up close to 6.4 expected across the tracked games. Against better forwards that leak becomes the story, and it is the clearest thing standing between them and a longer run. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Brazil, who worked 4.3 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 1 clean sheet from 3 games.

In this tournament the scoring has leaned on John McGinn, whose 1 make up over half of the group-stage goals. That is production and dependency in one number: mark him out and the supply can dry up.

## The key men

Individually, John McGinn has been the driver, 1 goal in 3 appearances at an average match rating of 6.7. Sides that lean on one player peak when he does and dip when he is quiet, and that dependence is worth watching in a knockout.

## What the numbers say

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers carry a warning: they were out-chanced more often than the table shows, and results built on that footing tend not to hold up against the best. A goal difference of -3 from 3 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

Set against the other 47 teams in the field, their attack ranks 32nd for expected goals created and 44th for goals scored, while the defence ranks 42nd for expected goals allowed. That is the truer placing, stripped of who they happened to draw, and it is the number to hold onto when the bracket starts throwing up mismatches and upsets.

## What's next

They are into the knockouts, where the 1745 rating and the xG edge matter less than holding a lead for ninety tense minutes.
