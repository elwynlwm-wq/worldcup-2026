On the FIFA ranking Senegal sit 18, and the Elo model has them at 1842, which frames them as a seeded side with real quality. Pape Thiaw's side were eliminated in the group stage, winning 1, drawing 0 and losing 2 of 3 games while scoring 8 and conceding 6. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

They are an attacking team first, happy to trade chances and back their forwards to win the exchange. Across the games tracked they averaged 1.78 expected goals for and 1.39 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they defeat 1-3 against France, and the expected goals (0.5 to 1.8) suggest they were second best on the run of play. No arguments about the result; they were beaten by the better side on the day. In their second group game they defeat 2-3 against Norway, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (1.7 to 2.2 on xG). A defeat that leaves questions without offering many answers. In their third group game they win 5-0 against Iraq, and the expected-goals split (3.1 to 0.2) says they made the better chances. The margin flattered no one; they were that far ahead.

In attack the finishing ran ahead of the process: 8 goals from roughly 5.3 expected. That is a fine line to walk, because clinical runs tend to regress, and a side relying on beating its xG is a side living slightly on credit. Their most threatening display came against Iraq, worth 3.1 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Sadio Mane (55), Ismaila Sarr (19), Nicolas Jackson (8), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

Defensively they were steady without being watertight, conceding 6 from around 4.2 expected. Solid enough to build on, not so solid that they can switch off. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Norway, who worked 2.2 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 1 clean sheet from 3 games.

The group-stage goals were spread around, Ismaïla Sarr (3) heading a list that also features Pape Gueye (2), Iliman Ndiaye (1), Ibrahim Mbaye (1). A side scoring from several places is a harder side to plan against.

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers sit close to even, the mark of a side that has to fight for every result rather than one that overwhelms. A goal difference of +2 from 0 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

Their tournament is done, and this profile is the record they leave behind, a campaign the numbers will remember more fairly than the results did.
