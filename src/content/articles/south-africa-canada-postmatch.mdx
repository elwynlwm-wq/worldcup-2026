We called this one a coin-flip, two sides who were not supposed to be here carrying more momentum than anyone expected, and for 90 minutes it played out exactly that way. Then Canada did the thing that has defined their summer: they won it in stoppage time.

Neither model nor market could separate these teams by much, and the match honoured that. South Africa, organised and full of running, gave Canada nothing easy, and for long spells the co-hosts' attack looked blunt, Jonathan David well marshalled, the tempo stodgy. This was not the free-flowing Canada of the 6-0 against Qatar. It was a grind, and a nervous one, in front of a crowd that sensed the ride might be about to stall.

It did not, because Stephen Eustáquio settled it in the 92nd minute, and with it Canada have their first knockout win in World Cup history. That is the headline, and it is enormous for a nation that had won a single World Cup match of any kind before this tournament. But the manner matters too. This is now twice in a row Canada have decided a knockout-stage game in stoppage time, and that is not luck, it is fitness and nerve, the mark of a side that believes it belongs.

The caveat is the one we flagged going in. Winning ugly against South Africa is one thing; the reward is Morocco, and that is a different examination entirely. Canada laboured to break down a well-drilled but limited side, and Morocco are far better drilled and considerably less limited. If David and, fitness permitting, Alphonso Davies cannot find more spark than they managed here, the next round will be a wall.

For today, though, none of that matters. Canada are into the last 16 of a home World Cup, further than they have ever been, on a night they will not forget. Jesse Marsch's team have made a nation believe, and belief, as they keep proving in stoppage time, is worth a goal.
