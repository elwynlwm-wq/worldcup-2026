Rated 2144 on Elo and 3 in the world, Spain arrived as one of the tournament favourites. Luis de la Fuente's side reached the knockout rounds, winning 2, drawing 1 and losing 0 of 3 games while scoring 5 and conceding 0. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

## How they play

This is a front-foot side that both makes chances and denies them, the profile of a team built to go deep. Across the games tracked they averaged 1.75 expected goals for and 0.18 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

## The campaign, game by game

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they drew 0-0 against Cape Verde, and the expected-goals split (2.1 to 0.2) says they made the better chances. Two points dropped more than one earned, on the balance of the chances. In their second group game they won 4-0 against Saudi Arabia, and they carried the greater threat, 2.3 to 0.1 on xG. A statement result that left little room for argument. In their third group game they won 1-0 against Uruguay, and the openings fell their way, 0.9 expected goals to 0.2. Not spectacular, but exactly the sort of result good sides grind out.

## Attack

In attack the output matched the process, 5 goals from around 5.3 expected, which is the healthiest place to be: winning on repeatable chance-creation rather than hot streaks. Their most threatening display came against Saudi Arabia, worth 2.3 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Ferran Torres (21), Mikel Oyarzabal (15), Dani Olmo (12), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

## Defence

Defensively they were miserly, conceding 0 and allowing only about 0.5 expected goals in the tracked games. A back line that gives up so little turns knockout football into a coin toss it can win from a single set piece. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Cape Verde, who worked 0.2 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 3 clean sheets from 3 games.

The group-stage goals were spread around, Mikel Oyarzabal (2) heading a list that also features Álex Baena (1), Lamine Yamal (1), Hassan Al-Tambakti (1). A side scoring from several places is a harder side to plan against.

## The key men

Individually, Mikel Oyarzabal has been the driver, 2 goals and 1 assist in 3 appearances at an average match rating of 7.17. Sides that lean on one player peak when he does and dip when he is quiet, and that dependence is worth watching in a knockout.

## What the numbers say

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers back the results: they created meaningfully more than they allowed, and that gap is usually the difference between a good run and an early flight home. A goal difference of +5 from 4 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

Set against the other 47 teams in the field, their attack ranks 13th for expected goals created and 19th for goals scored, while the defence ranks 1st for expected goals allowed. That is the truer placing, stripped of who they happened to draw, and it is the number to hold onto when the bracket starts throwing up mismatches and upsets.
