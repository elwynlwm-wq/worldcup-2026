Three games, three clean sheets, top of the group, not a goal conceded. On the scoreboard Spain have been flawless. Watch them, and you come away uneasy, and this is the day that unease gets its sternest examination.

Our model still makes Spain heavy favourites at 82%, and the market prices them at a warier 73%. Sit with that gap, because it is the match. The rating says a Euro champion with Lamine Yamal, Pedri and Rodri should stroll. The bookmakers, watching the same team draw 0-0 with Cape Verde and grind past Uruguay 1-0, have quietly decided Spain are more beatable than their pedigree suggests. We side with the bookmakers.

Luis de la Fuente's side have become strangely joyless. The defence is airtight, but the attack has been listless and almost entirely dependent on an 18-year-old who is reportedly carrying a knock. Rodri, so metronomic at his best, has been a step slow. And the timing could hardly be worse: Nico Williams and Yéremy Pino both limped out against Uruguay and are doubtful for the rest of the tournament, which strips de la Fuente of the wide runners who stretch a low block. A possession side that cannot get in behind is a possession side you can defend against.

Enter the worst possible opponent for a team in exactly this state. Ralf Rangnick has turned Austria into the purest expression of his football: a relentless, coordinated, high-risk press that hunts the ball the instant it is lost. Their group was chaos in both directions, roughly four goals a game, and they reached the knockouts on a 96th-minute Kalajdžić header against Algeria, their first World Cup knockout appearance since 1982. That is not the record of a great side. It is the record of a dangerous one.

The tactical collision is delicious. Spain's whole method is patient build-up from the back, and they have not faced a press this aggressive and this organised all tournament. Rangnick will send bodies at Rodri and the centre-backs, try to turn a serene passing exercise into a scrap, and back his fitness to win the second balls. If Spain's first pass out of pressure keeps sticking, they win comfortably and Austria's openness gets punished on the counter. If it does not, this becomes the upset the tournament has been threatening, and remember, Germany and the Netherlands are already gone.

The counter-argument for Spain is real and worth stating fairly: Austria are fragile in transition, they concede as freely as they score, and Spain do not need to be brilliant to win, they need one moment from Yamal or Mikel Oyarzabal and then the clean-sheet machine does the rest. That is the likeliest outcome. Our call is a Spain win, probably 1-0 or 2-1, uglier than the ranking, decided by a single piece of quality against a side that eventually over-commits.

But this is the most vulnerable of the big favourites, and if you are hunting a Round of 32 shock, start here rather than with Argentina or France. Austria have built the one style that asks Spain the question they have spent three games not answering, at the exact moment Spain lost two of their best answers to injury.

It is worth being specific about how Austria will try to break Spain, because the plan is triggers, not chaos. Rangnick's team wait for the pass into a full-back or a centre-back who has to turn, then swarm, cutting the return and forcing Spain long. Against most opponents Spain simply pass around it; the worry is that without Nico Williams and Pino stretching the pitch, everything funnels back through Rodri, and a Rodri who is a half-yard short is a pressing coach's dream. De la Fuente does have answers on the bench, Dani Olmo between the lines, Ferran Torres running in behind, but they are answers to a question Spain would rather not be asked at all.

The winner meets Portugal or Croatia in the last 16, another pairing of a stuttering favourite and a stubborn outsider, which means whoever comes through this could reach a quarter-final without ever hitting form. Spain should still be that team. On the balance of talent they get through. On the balance of what we have actually seen, keep the remote close.
