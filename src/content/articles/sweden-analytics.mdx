Rated 1742 on Elo and 36 in the world, Sweden arrived as a dangerous outsider. Graham Potter's side scraped through among the best third-placed sides, winning 1, drawing 1 and losing 2 of 4 games while scoring 7 and conceding 10. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

## How they play

They sit in the middle of the spectrum, neither dominant nor fragile, a side that has to earn everything it gets. Across the games tracked they averaged 0.91 expected goals for and 1.82 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

## The campaign, game by game

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they won 5-1 against Tunisia, and the expected-goals split (1.3 to 0.3) says they made the better chances. It was the kind of afternoon where everything they tried came off. In their second group game they lost 1-5 against Netherlands, and they were out-created, 1.0 to 2.6 on xG. ## Attack

In attack the finishing ran ahead of the process: 7 goals from roughly 3.6 expected. That is a fine line to walk, because clinical runs tend to regress, and a side relying on beating its xG is a side living slightly on credit. Their most threatening display came against Tunisia, worth 1.3 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Viktor Gyökeres (19), Alexander Isak (17), Anthony Elanga (6), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

## Defence

Defensively they were stretched, shipping 10 and giving up close to 7.3 expected across the tracked games. Against better forwards that leak becomes the story, and it is the clearest thing standing between them and a longer run. The heaviest pressure they faced came from France, who worked 3.2 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 0 clean sheets from 4 games.

The group-stage goals were spread around, Yasin Ayari (2) heading a list that also features Anthony Elanga (2), Viktor Gyökeres (1), Mattias Svanberg (1). A side scoring from several places is a harder side to plan against.

## The key men

Individually, Alexander Isak has been the driver, 1 goal and 3 assists in 4 appearances at an average match rating of 7. Sides that lean on one player peak when he does and dip when he is quiet, and that dependence is worth watching in a knockout.

## What the numbers say

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers carry a warning: they were out-chanced more often than the table shows, and results built on that footing tend not to hold up against the best. A goal difference of -3 from 4 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

Set against the other 47 teams in the field, their attack ranks 30th for expected goals created and 12th for goals scored, while the defence ranks 38th for expected goals allowed. That is the truer placing, stripped of who they happened to draw, and it is the number to hold onto when the bracket starts throwing up mismatches and upsets.
