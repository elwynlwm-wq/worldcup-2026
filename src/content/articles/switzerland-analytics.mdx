Rated 1914 on Elo and 16 in the world, Switzerland arrived as a seeded side with real quality. Murat Yakin's side topped their group, winning 2, drawing 1 and losing 0 of 3 games while scoring 7 and conceding 3. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

This is a front-foot side that both makes chances and denies them, the profile of a team built to go deep. Across the games tracked they averaged 2.12 expected goals for and 0.83 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they draw 1-1 against Qatar, and the expected-goals split (3.2 to 0.6) says they made the better chances. Two points dropped more than one earned, on the balance of the chances. In their second group game they win 4-1 against Bosnia and Herzegovina, and the expected-goals split (2.1 to 0.2) says they made the better chances. A statement result that left little room for argument. In their third group game they win 2-1 against Canada, and the chances were shared fairly evenly (1.1 to 1.7 on xG). The scoreline was kinder than the performance, and they will know it.

In attack the output matched the process, 7 goals from around 6.4 expected, which is the healthiest place to be: winning on repeatable chance-creation rather than hot streaks. Their most threatening display came against Qatar, worth 3.2 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Breel Embolo (24), Granit Xhaka (17), Remo Freuler (11), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

Defensively they were miserly, conceding 3 and allowing only about 2.5 expected goals in the tracked games. A back line that gives up so little turns knockout football into a coin toss it can win from a single set piece. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Canada, who worked 1.7 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 0 clean sheets from 3 games.

The group-stage goals were spread around, Johan Manzambi (3) heading a list that also features Rubén Vargas (2), Granit Xhaka (1), Breel Embolo (1). A side scoring from several places is a harder side to plan against. 2 of those were penalties, worth keeping in mind before reading too much into the open-play threat.

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers back the results: they created meaningfully more than they allowed, and that gap is usually the difference between a good run and an early flight home. A goal difference of +4 from 7 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

They are into the knockouts now, where the 1914 rating and the xG edge matter less than holding a lead for ninety tense minutes. On this evidence they have the tools; whether they have the nerve is the one thing no model prices.
