Rated 1914 on Elo and 16 in the world, Switzerland arrived as a seeded side with real quality. Murat Yakin's side topped their group, winning 3, drawing 2 and losing 0 of 5 games while scoring 9 and conceding 3. What follows is the tournament read off the data rather than the highlights reel.

## How they play

This is a front-foot side that both makes chances and denies them, the profile of a team built to go deep. Across the games tracked they averaged 2.23 expected goals for and 0.80 against, and the shape of that number, more than the results, is the honest measure of them.

## The campaign, game by game

Game by game, the campaign told its own story. In their opening group game they drew 1-1 against Qatar, and the expected-goals split (3.2 to 0.6) says they made the better chances. Two points dropped more than one earned, on the balance of the chances. In their second group game they won 4-1 against Bosnia and Herzegovina, and they carried the greater threat, 2.1 to 0.2 on xG. A statement result that left little room for argument. In their third group game they won 2-1 against Canada, and it stayed tight on xG, 1.1 to 1.7. The scoreline was kinder than the performance, and they will know it. In the round-of-32 tie they beat Algeria 2-0, and the expected-goals split (2.6 to 0.7) says they made the better chances. A win earned rather than gifted, and taken with the composure the occasion asked for. In the round-of-16 tie they were held to a 0-0 draw by Colombia and came through on penalties; a shootout is the bluntest way to settle a tie, but it still counts.

## Attack

In attack the output matched the process, 9 goals from around 8.9 expected, which is the healthiest place to be: winning on repeatable chance-creation rather than hot streaks. Their most threatening display came against Qatar, worth 3.2 xG on its own. The pedigree up front sits with Breel Embolo (24), Granit Xhaka (17), Remo Freuler (11), career international goals in brackets, the names that set the ceiling.

## Defence

Defensively they were miserly, conceding 3 and allowing only about 3.2 expected goals in the tracked games. A back line that gives up so little turns knockout football into a coin toss it can win from a single set piece. The heaviest pressure they faced came from Canada, who worked 1.7 xG against them, the game to learn most from. In total they kept 2 clean sheets from 5 games.

The group-stage goals were spread around, Johan Manzambi (3) heading a list that also features Rubén Vargas (2), Granit Xhaka (1), Breel Embolo (1). A side scoring from several places is a harder side to plan against. 2 of those were penalties, worth keeping in mind before reading too much into the open-play threat.

## The key men

Individually, Johan Manzambi has been the driver, 3 goals and 1 assist in 3 appearances at an average match rating of 7.63. Sides that lean on one player peak when he does and dip when he is quiet, and that dependence is worth watching in a knockout.

The team news matters too. Availability doubts hang over M. Muheim (calf muscle strain), and how many of those clear up before kickoff shapes what this side can actually put on the pitch.

## What the numbers say

Pulling it together, the underlying numbers back the results: they created meaningfully more than they allowed, and that gap is usually the difference between a good run and an early flight home. A goal difference of +6 from 7 group points is the surface record; the match-by-match expected-goals picture in the panel above is the version that travels.

Set against the other 47 teams in the field, their attack ranks 7th for expected goals created and 12th for goals scored, while the defence ranks 15th for expected goals allowed. That is the truer placing, stripped of who they happened to draw, and it is the number to hold onto when the bracket starts throwing up mismatches and upsets.
